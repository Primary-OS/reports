Cooling for AI hardware has crossed an uncomfortable threshold: it’s no longer a facilities line item, it’s a performance limiter and a reliability risk. As accelerator power climbs from the ~300–700W era into 1–2kW modules—and racks trend toward ~80–150kW density—the industry is discovering that “just add cold plates” doesn’t solve hotspots, service errors, and opaque failures that quietly erode GPU utilization. This report looks at whether there’s a startup Primary should incubate in this thermal management crisis, and lands on a clear answer: yes—but the most venture-scalable wedge is not another piece of plumbing. It’s a rack-level control, sensing, and reliability layer that makes liquid cooling operable at fleet scale and turns thermal stability into an SLA.

The takeaway is a concrete incubation thesis: build a “thermal control plane” that starts with leak/health detection (fastest ROI, lowest integration) and expands into closed-loop control and fleet benchmarking. Two-phase “thermal lid” hardware that eliminates hotspot throttling is the most technically exciting product in the space, but it’s slower, riskier, and more OEM-gated. The best company shape is a hardware-software system that can ride the adoption wave of direct-to-chip liquid now, accumulate the proprietary dataset that becomes defensible later, and sell to the fastest-moving buyers first: GPU clouds, colocation (multi-tenant data center) operators, and enterprise on-prem AI teams that can’t afford thermal incidents.

The customer reality: uptime, determinism, and serviceability—not “better cooling”

To evaluate incubation, it helps to picture the buyer’s day. The near-term volume of frontier AI compute is increasingly controlled by three groups:

GPU cloud operators (think “AI-first clouds”) scaling fleets quickly, often inside leased colocation space; colocation providers trying to win AI tenants by offering liquid-ready capacity; and OEM/ODM supply chains (server makers and Taiwanese original design manufacturers) that must ship manufacturable, supportable systems.

What these buyers are trying to do is simple to say and hard to execute: run extremely expensive GPUs at high utilization with predictable performance and minimal downtime. In practice, that means maintaining stable junction temperatures (the temperature at the silicon) under spiky workloads, swapping failed components quickly, and avoiding tail-risk events—especially leaks—that can take down a rack and trigger painful warranty and replacement cycles.

This framing matters because it shifts “cooling” from a thermodynamics problem to an operations product. The winners will be the vendors who reduce incident rates and service time, not just those who can publish a better heat-transfer coefficient.

Where current approaches break: hotspots, human error, and “ghost failures”

In today’s deployments, direct-to-chip liquid cooling (cold plates attached to the chip with coolant flowing through them) is moving from special-case to default at the high end. But operators consistently run into five recurring failure modes:

First, hotspots cause throttling even when average temperatures look fine. Modern packages combine compute, high-bandwidth memory (HBM), and dense power delivery; localized hot zones can drop clocks, creating performance jitter and unpredictable training times.

Second, serviceability is brutal. Liquid loops introduce hoses, quick disconnects, manifolds, pumps, filters, and coolant distribution units (CDUs—systems that pump and manage coolant inside or near the rack). Every additional connector increases mean time to repair (MTTR) and raises the odds of technician error.

Third, leak risk near high-value GPUs remains psychologically and economically unacceptable. Even a small leak can cascade into downtime, hardware damage, and weeks of operational distraction.

Fourth, thermals are hard to observe end-to-end. Telemetry is fragmented across GPU sensors, the baseboard management controller (BMC—server management firmware), the CDU, and facility systems. Slow degradation modes—fouling, pump wear, trapped gas, micro-leaks, thermal interface material (TIM) pump-out—often look like “ghosts” until performance drops or an alarm triggers.

Finally, facilities are constrained on heat rejection. Even if you can cool the chip, many sites can’t efficiently move heat out of the building due to chilled-water capacity, ambient temperature, or water constraints.

The common thread is that the pain isn’t only heat transfer—it’s operating a liquid-cooled fleet with the predictability of air cooling.

Why now: the control-plane moment has arrived

Two changes make this a timing window rather than an evergreen “cooling is important” observation.

The first is the demand shock. AI buildouts are compressing deployment timelines: rack density is rising faster than facilities can be redesigned, and liquid adoption is accelerating because air cooling simply runs out of headroom at the frontier.

The second is technical: dense sensing, edge compute, and control algorithms are finally cheap and reliable enough to turn cooling into a real-time control problem. GPU telemetry is richer than it used to be (power, clocks, throttling behavior), and the sensor surface area in liquid systems (flow, pressure, inlet/outlet temps, conductivity) is naturally higher. Continuous leak and contamination detection has matured from periodic checks to plausible early warning.

That combination—liquid becoming default, plus better observability and actuation—creates an opening for a new “operating layer” to become the standard before incumbents lock the data exhaust inside proprietary stacks.

The opportunity: three startup shapes, one likely winner

The market structure points to three credible venture-scale concepts. They differ in risk, timeline, and defensibility—but they’re complementary enough to share an incubation roadmap.

ThermaOS [working name] — Make liquid cooling operable at fleet scale.

  • Who it serves: GPU cloud operators in colocation, hyperscale colocation providers, and enterprise on-prem AI teams running liquid-cooled racks.
  • Wedge: A “Thermal SLA kit”—sensor fabric + rack controller + software—that lands first as leak/health early warning and incident workflow, then expands into closed-loop optimization (pump/valve/setpoint control) and benchmarking across racks.
  • Moat: Cornered resource (a proprietary, labeled dataset of thermal incidents and precursors across many deployments) plus switching costs (embedded runbooks, tuned thresholds, and operational history).
  • Key risk: Being dismissed as “nice-to-have software” unless bundled with control authority and tied to explicit economics (downtime avoided, higher allowable coolant temps, fewer truck rolls, higher sustained clocks).

LeakGuard — Warranty-grade leak and coolant health detection.

  • Who it serves: Enterprise and GPU clouds that fear rare but catastrophic incidents; colos offering SLAs to tenants.
  • Wedge: Bolt-on sensing (conductivity/pressure signature/flow anomalies, potentially optical/humidity where relevant) plus edge inference that detects micro-leaks, cavitation, contamination, and drift early and recommends mitigation actions.
  • Moat: Data compounding (failure signatures get better with each deployment) plus workflow embed; strong trust brand if it prevents even a handful of incidents.
  • Key risk: Feature capture by CDU or server vendors bundling “good enough” sensors; must win on cross-vendor integration and fewer false positives.

VaporPlate — Two-phase, hotspot-proof package-level cooling.

  • Who it serves: OEMs/ODMs and hyperscaler hardware teams designing next-gen high-TDP GPU platforms.
  • Wedge: A sealed two-phase micro-evaporator “thermal lid” that flattens hotspot gradients across compute, HBM, and power-delivery zones to prevent throttling at extreme heat flux.
  • Moat: Switching costs once qualified into a platform, plus process power from yield/reliability learning curves and specialized manufacturing.
  • Key risk: Qualification and perception risk. Two-phase designs introduce fears about long-term stability (dry-out, orientation sensitivity, seal integrity). A single high-profile failure can freeze adoption.

A simple way to see the relationship: the control plane is the scalable business; leak detection is the fastest landing product; two-phase is the moonshot hardware bet that may become necessary when single-phase cold plates hit a hard wall.

The two-phase module is worth tracking—and potentially seeding as a parallel R&D thread—but it is less forgiving, more capital intensive, and slower to revenue.

Recommendation: incubate the control plane, not another cold plate

Primary should incubate a rack-level thermal control and reliability company: a hardware-software system that can be installed into existing liquid-cooled racks, prove value quickly through incident prevention and better operational visibility, and then expand into closed-loop optimization and cross-fleet benchmarking. The founding team profile is critical: a technical CEO or CTO with credibility in controls/reliability engineering and datacenter operations (not just thermal simulation), plus a product lead who can sell “SLA outcomes” to operators rather than features to engineers.

The first move is to secure 2–3 design partners where downtime economics are explicit and integration barriers are manageable—GPU clouds in colocation are the cleanest wedge—then ship an initial “Leak + Health” module that (1) reduces incident risk, (2) generates labeled telemetry, and (3) earns permission to control setpoints over time. If we wait, the dataset and operational workflows that create defensibility will likely get trapped inside hyperscaler internal tooling or incumbent CDU stacks, turning a future entrant into a feature supplier instead of the system of record.
