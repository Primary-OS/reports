Enterprise AI has crossed an uncomfortable threshold: there’s enough model capability to automate meaningful work, and enough adoption that “a few pilots” has turned into a messy, expensive, and risky estate of copilots, agents, and API keys. In a recent conversation with Manik, the same theme surfaced that we’re hearing broadly: companies can’t answer basic questions—where is AI being used, is it safe, what is it costing, and what is it returning? This report examines whether a startup can become the missing control plane that makes AI governable and profitable at enterprise scale—and what, specifically, we would incubate.

The conclusion: there is a startup worth incubating, but not as yet another “governance dashboard.” The defensible opening is to become the in-line runtime decision point for AI traffic—an enterprise gateway that can enforce policy, instrument outcomes, and (critically) optimize cost and performance across models. If we build it so that it becomes part of audit evidence and financial reporting—not just engineering convenience—switching becomes a compliance project, and the data exhaust becomes a compounding asset.

The customer is not “the enterprise”—it’s a three-way knife fight inside it

Control planes sound abstract until you picture the people who actually feel the pain. In most large organizations, AI adoption is happening in parallel across at least three groups, each with different definitions of “success.”

Security and compliance leaders (the Chief Information Security Officer, or CISO, plus governance/risk/compliance teams) worry about data leakage, retention, and auditability—especially when employees paste sensitive text into public chat tools or when agents pull from internal knowledge bases. Platform and engineering leaders are trying to ship reliable AI features without building bespoke infrastructure for identity, logging, approvals, and evaluation. Finance—often a mix of the Chief Financial Officer (CFO), financial planning and analysis (FP&A), and FinOps (cloud cost management)—is now staring at token bills and seat licenses with no credible way to tie spend to outcomes.

This organizational structure matters because it points to the buying dynamic: the winning control plane is the one that can satisfy all three constituencies at once, or at least create dependency across them. A tool used only by an AI engineering team stays optional. A tool referenced in audit controls and chargeback processes becomes hard to displace.

Why today’s approaches keep breaking in production

Enterprises have not been idle. They’ve tried bans, “approved chat” tools, internal agent prototypes, and a growing pile of point solutions. The failure modes, however, repeat.

The first is shadow AI: usage spreads through browser extensions, personal accounts, unsanctioned SaaS copilots, and one-off internal scripts. Bans get bypassed; sanctioned chat doesn’t cover real workflow automation; and leadership loses visibility into what data is flowing where.

The second is quality regression. Prompts change, models update, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG—LLMs that fetch relevant internal documents at query time) indices drift, and suddenly accuracy drops or hallucinations spike. Most teams still rely on ad hoc manual testing because “LLM behavior” is probabilistic, and traditional software tests don’t map cleanly.

The third is data governance brittleness in RAG: permissions and entitlements don’t reliably propagate from systems like SharePoint, Google Drive, or Confluence into the retrieval layer. Teams respond by over-restricting access or creating duplicative indices, which kills usefulness and adoption.

The fourth is cost without unit economics. Enterprises can see token spend and seat counts, but not “cost per resolved support ticket” or “cost per contract reviewed,” making it impossible to manage AI like any other operational investment.

Finally, there’s the unglamorous blocker: productionization. The demo works in a week; the real deployment needs single sign-on (SSO), role-based access control (RBAC), audit logs, change control, incident response, and approvals. Without a shared layer, every team rebuilds the same plumbing—slowly and inconsistently.

All of this sets up the key insight: a control plane doesn’t win by offering more visibility. It wins by becoming the place where AI access is routed, decided, and recorded.

Why now: models became “doers,” and enterprises need an adult in the loop

Two technical shifts make a real control plane newly viable.

First, frontier models now support reliable tool use and structured outputs (for example, producing valid JSON that downstream systems can validate). That means AI can take actions inside real systems—create a ticket, update a customer record, change an entitlement—rather than just draft text. Once AI acts, governance must move from PDFs to runtime enforcement.

Second, the ecosystem is now truly multi-model (OpenAI, Anthropic, cloud-hosted models, open-source). Price and performance change monthly. Enterprises increasingly want optionality, but each model integration is different, and optimizing across them is nontrivial. That creates an opening for a neutral layer whose business model aligns with the customer’s economics—especially around cost and performance.

This timing window won’t stay open. Hyperscalers and major enterprise suites will add governance features. The standalone startup that survives is the one that becomes indispensable in the runtime path and accumulates the telemetry dataset that others can’t replicate.

What we’d build: three concepts, one clear incubation favorite

The market supports multiple “control plane” shapes. But Primary should bias toward the one that (a) has a sharp initial wedge, (b) becomes embedded in critical workflows, and (c) compounds defensibility through data and switching costs.

RouterBench — A neutral routing layer that cuts AI spend 20–60% while enforcing quality and latency targets.

  • Who it serves: AI platform teams and procurement/FinOps leaders at enterprises with meaningful model spend and multiple teams shipping AI.
  • Wedge: An in-line gateway that benchmarks requests, routes them to the cheapest model that meets a defined quality/service-level agreement (SLA), and applies caching and fallbacks.
  • Moat: Counter-positioning plus emerging switching costs. Model vendors and clouds are disincentivized to optimize customers away from high-margin usage; once routing policies and performance history become operationally trusted, removal becomes risky.
  • Key risk: Routing can commoditize (open-source gateways, cloud features). The company must “own quality truth” with credible evaluation and outcome instrumentation, not just cost optimization.

PolicyMesh — The “AI firewall” that enforces identity-aware policy at runtime across models, agents, and tool calls.

  • Who it serves: CISOs and governance/risk/compliance teams, with platform engineering as the implementation partner; strongest pull in regulated industries.
  • Wedge: Centralized policy-as-code enforcement: data redaction, connector allowlists, retention rules, approvals for sensitive actions, immutable audit logs.
  • Moat: Switching costs if it becomes the policy decision point plus audit trail; over time, a “trust brand” can matter in security buying.
  • Key risk: A crowded “AI security” landscape and bundling by Microsoft, Google, AWS, and security suites. Without a uniquely enforceable runtime position, it risks becoming a replaceable dashboard.

LedgerAI — A finance-grade system of record that ties every AI event to cost, owner, and business outcome.

  • Who it serves: CFO/FP&A and CIO/enterprise apps leaders trying to govern AI as spend.
  • Wedge: Cross-vendor cost attribution (across copilots, model APIs, and internal agents), chargeback, and ROI instrumentation at the workflow level.
  • Moat: Switching costs once embedded in budgeting and audit; cornered resource if it captures normalized cost/quality/outcome telemetry across enterprises.
  • Key risk: ROI attribution is politically and technically messy; without tight integration into workflows, it becomes “pretty dashboards” finance doesn’t trust.

To clarify how these layers relate, the winning strategy is to sit on the critical path—where requests flow and actions happen—then expand upward into governance evidence and ROI.

The moat we can actually build: system-of-record, not feature checklist

The Seven Powers lens is blunt here: “enterprise AI control plane” is not defensible by default. Branding is weak early, generic connectors are copyable, and hyperscalers will ship overlapping admin features. The path to durable power is sequential.

First, we must earn switching costs by becoming the runtime enforcement point and the audit trail for AI activity—meaning a large share of governed AI calls route through us, and compliance teams reference us in internal controls. Second, we build a cornered resource: normalized telemetry across prompts, tool calls, policy decisions, evaluation results, and—most importantly—workflow outcomes. That dataset is what eventually enables better auto-remediation, policy recommendations, and benchmarking than any new entrant can match.

A useful internal milestone to watch for product-market pull is whether a customer becomes dependent across functions: security relies on it for audit evidence, engineering relies on it for release confidence, and finance relies on it for attribution. That’s when “optional tool” becomes “enterprise control point.”

Recommendation: incubate RouterBench, but design it to grow into the system of record

Primary should incubate a neutral AI gateway with routing as the initial wedge, built explicitly to expand into policy enforcement and ROI instrumentation. Cost savings is the fastest credible reason to centralize traffic; once centralized, governance and audit become enforceable, and ROI becomes measurable. The company we want is not “the best dashboard,” but the layer that enterprises must route through to safely run agents and copilots at scale.

The founder profile that fits: a technical product leader who has lived enterprise platform adoption (identity, logging, SOC 2, procurement cycles) and can sell to a three-headed buyer (platform, security, finance). The first move is to validate two things in design-partner conversations: (1) willingness to route production traffic through a third-party gateway, and (2) a measurable savings or standardization goal big enough to overcome bundling pressure.

If those validate, the 12-month product bet is clear: own the runtime path, ship verified savings, and accumulate the audit-grade telemetry that makes replacement painful.
